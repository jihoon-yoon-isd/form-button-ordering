Button Ordering in Multi-Step Forms

Introduction

The dilemma

What order do we place Continue/Previous buttons on a multi-step form page?

There are two standards that seem to be at odds here when considering the ordering of buttons on an inline button set:

  1. Primary button should appear before (to the left of) the secondary button
  2. Previous and Next buttons should follow semantic directional ordering

So the question is, if Primary buttons should stay left, but previous/back buttons should also stay left, which one is correct?

Here are discussions on this specific dilemma, preferring sources that specifically mention both cases and take a side.

Evidence

Twilio Paste Design System

Twilio's Paste design system explicitly distinguishes between form types. The primary/secondary ordering rule is situational, recommended for single-step pages and not multi-step forms (like SFP).

The primary button placement depends on the form type:
  • For Single Step Forms, Side Modals, Side Panels, Minimizable Dialogs and Popover forms, keep the primary button left-aligned, and placed before secondary buttons for better scannability of the content and its related actions.
  • For Multi-step Forms and Wizards, keep the primary button right-aligned, placed after secondary buttons to indicate the direction of the form. Refer to our Wizards action footer guidelines for additional guidance.

Source: Twilio's Twilio Paste design system  ·  paste.twilio.design/patterns/form

IBM Carbon Design System

IBM's Carbon Design System also advises context dependent ordering, not one universal rule. They state that for multi-page forms, the primary forward action should be placed to the right of the secondary action, but for full/single page form designs, the primary button appears on the left of the secondary/tertiary actions.

As a general rule, on full-page designs, the primary button is on the left side of the page.
Whereas in wizards, where a user is progressing through a series of steps or dialog windows, the primary action traditionally sits at the bottom right.

Source: IBM's Carbon Design System  ·  v10.carbondesignsystem.com/components/button/usage

PatternFly (Red Hat)

PatternFly reinforces the primary/secondary standard:

The Next button should always be a primary button. The Back button should always be a secondary button.

They say this for vertically stacked buttons (mobile, narrow viewport):

Responsiveness: The most important actions (i.e., primary buttons) are encountered first when elements are stacked vertically.

However, for our specific dilemma, they also have the multi-page form buttons in directional order.

Source: PatternFly, an open source design system maintained by Red Hat  ·  patternfly.org/components/button/design-guidelines/

JP Morgan Salt Design System

JP Morgan's Salt Design System supports the directional order argument for multi-page forms. It separates importance from spatial order, saying that the next button is primary visually, but the order follows the sequence of movement through the form.

Always position Next right of Previous and Cancel to reflect the direction of the steps.

The consideration would change for single forms or button sets that don't have implicit directional meaning, such as Submit and Cancel.

Source: JP Morgan's Salt Design System  ·  saltdesignsystem.com/salt/patterns/button-bar

VA Design System (US Department of Veterans Affairs)

VA.gov is the site for the US Department of Veterans Affairs. The VA Design System is the design guidance for their digital products. They specifically state multiple times in the reference below that primary should appear before secondary.

Button groups within large modals stack vertically at full width until the tablet breakpoint. Above that breakpoint, buttons revert to their natural width and appear inline, with the primary button before the secondary button.
At and above the mobile-lg breakpoint, buttons left-align and revert to their natural width (i.e., as wide as they need to be to accommodate their label). The primary button continues to appear before the secondary button.

However, even they differentiate between this and the specific scenario of back/continue buttons.

Use the Update/Cancel button group variation for saving form input on a form.
Use the Back/Continue button group variation for providing navigation options through a step by step flow of form pages.

Source: VA Design System  ·  design.va.gov/components/button/button-group

Adam Silver (designer and author of Form Design Patterns)

Adam Silver argues that putting the back button to the left of the primary continue button is the research-backed preferred way to group these buttons:

Research carried out by Mick Couper, Reg Baker and Joanne Mechling shows that placing the back button to the right of the primary button is confusing and it should go either to the left or underneath instead.

However, he also notes the nuance of using directional buttons when it makes intuitive and semantic sense. For continue/cancel or continue/save, it would not be the same.

Source: Adam Silver  ·  adamsilver.io/blog/where-to-put-buttons-on-forms/

Luke Wroblewski

Luke Wroblewski argues for putting primary on the left because users rarely go backward, and a prominent Continue button helps them get through the form quickly:

In the vast majority of Web forms, people just want to get through the questions they need to answer and move on. Therefore having a primary action that is "Continue" and a secondary action that is "Go Back" makes a lot of sense.
a lot of the analytics I have seen show people very rarely utilize 'go back'

However, the Bergstrom study below directly tested this efficiency argument. Even participants who got the more efficient primary-left layout still preferred the conventional directional one.

Source: Luke Wroblewski  ·  lukew.com/ff/entry.asp?730

User Opinions

PatternFly Community Forum (2019)

In the PatternFly forums, a user asked this question amid disagreement about the ordering of the next/previous button in multi-page forms/wizards.

The ordering of the buttons seems contrary and opposite to every general UI pattern I've seen for button layout in wizards. Is this intentional, or is this some kind of L-to-R from R-to-L conversion mistake? Has this placement been tested?

The response from one of the two team members that responded was:

The intention is to keep the button placement consistent for all use cases - forms, modals, wizards, etc. The primary button is the first / left-most button. In a wizard context it's 'next'.

However, every single response from the users was strongly in disagreement:

I agree with Mairin, the current order is weird and counterintuitive.
Perhaps the largest offense is reversing the order of Next | Back | Cancel in the wizard component. Why is PatternFly digging in on the basis that the primary action must always come first, if it breaks how we comprehend progression (beginning : end :: left : right)?
By this reasoning, PatternFly breaks it's own rule in being universal as the web browsers' own navigation buttons appear as Back | Forward, not the other way around.
Conventions for button placement have been well established, tested, and remain relevant to this day. Why, again, are we deciding to break away from them?
For 25+ years users have been trained 'previous' is left, 'next' is right in multi page / multi tab / wizard navigation…
When implementing the new onboarding experience for CodeReady Containers we also used the Wizard and were amazed about this ordering as it is not suitable for a desktop implementation, like running React+Electron on Windows or macOS.
We asked for feedback from the community and stakeholders using a video... and it was the first feedback we got most often.
Looking at the Wizard guidelines this is intentional. But it breaks with the mental model that exists on the desktop.
[this] breaks the pattern of every other wizard users might encounter where the primary action button is in the lower right. The model for a wizard is a linear left-to-right path, like flipping through the pages of a book - you can't flip forward by striking the page at the binding?

Source: forum.patternfly.org/t/wizard-button-placement/228/17

Note: this discussion was from 2019, but the updated standards above show that they now recommend directional form buttons.

Research

A stronger, more scientific piece of evidence is from a study titled "Navigation Buttons in Web-Based Surveys: Respondents' Preferences Revisited in the Laboratory" by Bergstrom, Erdman, and Lakh, published in 2016.

Participants completed a web survey in one of two conditions:

  • "NP" — Next left, Previous right
  • "PN" — Previous left, Next right

They measured completion, satisfaction, negative/positive comments, and eye tracking.

Satisfaction

The PN condition scored significantly higher:

NP Condition
Next left
4.79/9
PN Condition
Next right
7.73/9
Preference

39 out of 41 participants preferred the PN setup (Next to the right of Previous).

Negative Comments

Users made significantly more negative comments about the NP pattern (Next on the left).

Direct quotes from NP (Next-left) participants:

  • "the Next button was on the opposite side of the screen from where I expected it"
  • "The placement of the Next and Previous buttons did not make sense to me even though I did not have to move my mouse as far to get to the Next button"
  • "almost all the other surveys I've completed have Next on the right and Previous on the left…it's like reading a book"

Direct quotes from PN (Previous-left, Next-right) participants:

  • "the buttons were in the right place"
  • "where they normally would be on an Internet form"
  • "made sense"
  • "logical, visible and accessible"

The authors' own conclusions:

Web survey designers should be aware that peoples' interactions with Web-based products are influenced by their interactions with other daily products such as remote controls, telephones, and books... Designers should not re-invent the wheel or expect users to re-learn their mental models that have taken years of experience to form.

Source: surveypractice.org — Bergstrom, Erdman & Lakh (2016)

Conclusion

The internal concern was about keeping the primary button placement consistent throughout the entire form. Currently:

  • Primary on the right (previous → continue)
  • Primary on the left (submit → cancel)

But the question is whether this is a real consistency problem worth breaking the directional convention to solve. The evidence above suggests it isn't.

Submit/Cancel and Previous/Next aren't really the same kind of button pair, and several sources above advise directional buttons specifically for multi-step forms even while strongly recommending primary-left for other contexts. They treat these as two different scenarios, and mixing them up for the sake of consistency seems like an unnecessary hit to usability.

Yes-No (submit/cancel) and Previous-Next are both near-universally agreed upon natural orderings (at least in the western world), so changing either of them would create an inconsistency with expectations.

I strongly believe we should go with the natural directional button ordering for the multi-page forms, as that was the guidance of the strong majority of the sources I found. Even if we were to disregard those guidelines entirely, it seems as if putting the Continue button on the left would annoy far more users than if it were the other way around.

Considerations

I found that for mobile, guidelines almost always advised primary top, secondary below. This makes sense to me, since vertically stacked buttons do not have horizontal form meaning attached to them. Our discussion is about inline button ordering.

A common factor I saw while doing this research was button alignment (left vs right aligned on the page). This adds another dimension to the argument, but I don't think it would change any conclusions, because the main argument for the non-direction button ordering is consistency/accessibility, and both cases are not affected by alignment.

Another alternative I found was to change the placement of the back button, such as to the top of the page.

For screen readers, I don't think it would be a concern. Screen reader users are listening for what is spoken, not memorizing spatial orderings. Screen readers also announce the content of the button before its role, so it would read: "BACK, button, main" (what it reads currently on SFP).
